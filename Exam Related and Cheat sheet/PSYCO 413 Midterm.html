Background about this data:

The data “HumanNearMiss.csv” contains the results of a study that tested the hypothesis that near-miss events on slot machines (e.g., receiving a “cherry →→ cherry →→ lemon” reel pattern) reinforce a person’s tendency to keep gambling. Two treatments were compared (given by the “treatment” column):

  • Near-Miss: a group with a high frequency of “near-miss” reel patterns
  • Far-Miss: a group with a high-frequency of “far-miss” (“lemon →→ cherry →→ cherry”) reel patterns. This group served as a control condition.

If near-misses have a reinforcing effect on behaviour (i.e., if they increase people’s tendency to keep playing), the near-miss group would be expected to show higher amounts of responding (given by the “responses” column) than the far-miss treatment group during a period of extinction (when no wins were given).

  • Pisklak, J. M., Yong, J. J. H., & Spetch, M. L. (2020). The Near-Miss Effect in Slot Machines: A Review and Experimental Analysis Over Half a Century Later. Journal of Gambling Studies, 36(2), 611–632. https://doi.org/10.1007/s10899-019-09891-8
